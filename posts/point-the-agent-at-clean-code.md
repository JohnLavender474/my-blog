"Point the agent at clean, intentional patterns, and it generates clean, intentional code." I've seen and heard quotes like this recently.

Besides the fact that what constitutes "clean" and "intentional" can be subjective in many cases, there's also the reality of drift: error and inconsistency get introduced (whether by human, by agent, or some other force) into the system, and over time things drift into an imperfect or even broken state.

I don't think this phenomenon is unique to coding agents; it's just the reality of building complex systems in the real world. That's why ongoing and proactive planning, guidance, review, and learning are so necessary. As the system and requirements change, the process needs to adapt — we need to adapt.

Your agent workflow may not be appropriate for the given task. Your beautifully written skill files may mislead the agent and burn many tokens along the way. Your "clean code" instructions don't mean much when those instructions get lost in the model context and the model ends up outright breaking those rules. Sure, you can use "LLM as judge" or "LLM as orchestrator" to assuage these issues, but has that perfectly panned out for anyone yet?

Coding agents so far haven't solved the process; they've just sped it up and made it more capable. But don't mistake capability with correctness. Put a coding agent into the hands of someone who knows what correct looks like, and the drift can stay minimized, though never truly vanished. But put one into the hands of someone who doesn't know what correct looks like, and all that capability and power will lead to a total mess in no time that even the best coding agents will struggle to contend with.
