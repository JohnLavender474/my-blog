Recently, I watched a video by ThePrimeagen titled "What is wrong with us?!" in which he talks through his experiences with AI-assisted programming, not from a technical angle but rather from a personal, introspective one. The feelings he describes throughout ring true for me. Here's a compressed version of what he said:

> *Going back to hand-coding felt wrong — vibe coding is faster, but I hate the code it produces. So I kept spinning up agents. The ideas kept coming because MVPs are so easy to start. That's the Faustian bargain: what begins as a few words balloons into endless prompting, longer cycles, more agents running at once.*
>
> *You're constantly babysitting small steps, translating fixes into English prompts, starting new things because starting is so cheap. I'm building more than I ever have — but I'm not sure I'm accomplishing anything.*
>
> *The work multiplies. The juggling spirals. Productivity is through the roof, and nothing really happens.*

Until 8 or 9 months ago, I didn't use AI tools much. The results from AI weren't good enough for the level of rigor I try to hold myself to when writing code and designing features and systems. I was happy to clack away code at the keyboard the old-fashioned way.

But the transition happened eventually. With models and harnesses as robust as they are now, there's no escaping it: the tools are changing the nature of the industry I studied for and work in.

Nowadays, if I need to build a feature in the frontend, it'd be irresponsible not to just hand that off to an agent and simply review the results when they're done. If I need to plan out some complicated model relationships and system design in the backend, it'd be ludicrous not to at least try to hand that off to Claude — planning, implementation, the works. If I need to do something that seems at first so simple that I could type it out in 10 seconds in a couple of lines of code, well... I'd better prompt the AI for its opinion first, right? What if it sees something I don't? Or just hand the task off entirely.

AI doesn't do all the work for me, nor should it. There are times when handing something off to an agent creates more work than it saves, and the results are weaker for it. The real trap is that it's so easy to let AI take the first few steps that you don't always notice when maybe you should have taken them yourself.

What's the resolution here? Or have we just sped up the same processes, trials, and errors that were already there?

To clarify, I don't enjoy handing off tasks to the agent without having a deep understanding of the codebase, systems, and requirements that I'm working in.

That said, there's a tension I keep coming back to. If I can describe exactly what needs to be done in plain language, at what point does writing or even understanding the code myself become the less rational choice? And if the AI doesn't yield what I expected, or if I have qualms with its methods and implementation details, then whose fault is it? Its fault or mine?
